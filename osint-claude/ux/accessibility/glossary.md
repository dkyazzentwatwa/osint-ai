# OSINT Glossary

A comprehensive A-Z guide to OSINT terms in simple language.

---

## How to Use This Glossary

**Quick Lookup:**
- Type `/glossary [term]` to jump to definition
- Type `/glossary` to browse all terms
- Terms are cross-referenced for related concepts

**Reading Level:**
- Definitions written at 8th-grade reading level
- Technical terms explained within definitions
- Examples provided for each term

---

## A

**API (Application Programming Interface)**  
A way for different computer programs to talk to each other. Like a waiter taking your order to the kitchen and bringing back food.  
*Example: Using the Twitter API to search for posts programmatically.*

**Archived Data**  
Old information that has been saved and stored, often from websites that no longer exist or have changed.  
*Example: Viewing a website as it looked in 2015 using the Wayback Machine.*

**Asset**  
Anything of value owned by a person or company, including websites, social media accounts, or digital files.  
*Example: A company's domains, trademarks, and social media pages are all assets.*

**Authentication**  
The process of proving who you are or that something is genuine.  
*Example: Logging in with a password, or verifying a photo is real and not fake.*

---

## B

**Background Check**  
Research into a person's history, including employment, education, and public records.  
*Example: Verifying someone's resume claims before hiring them.*

**Breadcrumb**  
A navigation trail showing where you are in a system and how you got there.  
*Example: Home > Investigations > Results > Photo Analysis*

**Bug Bounty**  
A program where companies pay people to find security problems in their systems.  
*Example: Google paying researchers who find vulnerabilities in their websites.*

---

## C

**Cache**  
A stored copy of a webpage saved by search engines or browsers for quick access.  
*Example: Viewing Google's cached version of a website that is currently offline.*

**Catfish**  
Someone who pretends to be someone else online, usually using fake photos and information.  
*Example: A scammer using a model's photos to start romantic relationships.*

**Crawl / Crawler**  
An automated program that visits websites and collects information. Also called a spider or bot.  
*Example: Googlebot crawling websites to add them to search results.*

**Cross-Reference**  
Comparing information from multiple sources to verify accuracy.  
*Example: Checking that someone's LinkedIn job history matches their company website bio.*

**CVE (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures)**  
A standard way to identify and track security problems in software.  
*Example: CVE-2021-44228 refers to the Log4j vulnerability.*

---

## D

**Data Breach**  
When private information is accidentally or illegally released to unauthorized people.  
*Example: A hacker stealing a company's customer email list and passwords.*

**Digital Footprint**  
The trail of information you leave behind when using the internet.  
*Example: Your social media posts, online purchases, and website comments.*

**Directory**  
A folder or location where files are stored on a computer or website.  
*Example: A website's /images/ directory containing all photos.*

**DNS (Domain Name System)**  
The phone book of the internet that converts website names to computer addresses.  
*Example: Converting google.com to the IP address 142.250.80.46.*

**Dork / Google Dork**  
A special search query using advanced operators to find specific information.  
*Example: Searching `site:gov filetype:pdf "confidential"` to find government PDFs.*

**Domain**  
The address of a website, like example.com.  
*Example: amazon.com, wikipedia.org, google.com are all domains.*

---

## E

**Email Header**  
The hidden technical information attached to every email showing its path and origin.  
*Example: Headers can reveal the true sender, not just the displayed "From" address.*

**Encryption**  
Scrambling information so only authorized people can read it.  
*Example: HTTPS websites encrypt data between you and the site.*

**EXIF Data**  
Hidden information stored in photos, including camera type, date taken, and GPS location.  
*Example: A photo containing GPS coordinates showing where it was taken.*

**Exposure**  
When private or sensitive information is accidentally made public.  
*Example: A database left accessible on the internet without a password.*

---

## F

**FCRA (Fair Credit Reporting Act)**  
A US law regulating how background check information can be used for employment and housing.  
*Example: Requiring consent before running a credit check on a job applicant.*

**Favicon**  
The small icon that appears in browser tabs next to a website's name.  
*Example: The blue "f" icon for Facebook, or colorful "G" for Google.*

**Forensics**  
The scientific analysis of digital evidence to uncover facts.  
*Example: Examining a photo's metadata to determine if it's been edited.*

**FTP (File Transfer Protocol)**  
A method for moving files between computers over the internet.  
*Example: Uploading website files to a hosting server.*

---

## G

**Geolocation**  
Identifying the real-world location of something using digital information.  
*Example: Finding where a photo was taken using GPS coordinates in the image.*

**Geotag**  
Location information attached to photos, videos, or posts.  
*Example: A tweet showing it was posted from "New York, NY."*

---

## H

**Hash**  
A unique digital fingerprint of a file or piece of data.  
*Example: Using a photo's hash to find identical copies online.*

**Header (HTTP)**  
Technical information sent between your browser and websites.  
*Example: Security headers that protect against certain attacks.*

**Honeypot**  
A trap set to detect or deflect unauthorized access attempts.  
*Example: A fake vulnerable server that logs hacker attempts.*

**Hostname**  
The name assigned to a device connected to a network.  
*Example: www.example.com, mail.google.com, ftp.files.com.*

**HTTPS**  
The secure version of HTTP that encrypts data between browser and website.  
*Example: Looking for the padlock icon in your browser address bar.*

---

## I

**IOC (Indicator of Compromise)**  
Evidence that a security breach or incident has occurred.  
*Example: Finding malicious files, suspicious IP addresses, or unusual login patterns.*

**IP Address**  
A unique number assigned to every device connected to the internet.  
*Example: 192.168.1.1 or 203.0.113.45.*

---

## J

**JSON (JavaScript Object Notation)**  
A simple format for storing and sharing data that is easy for computers to read.  
*Example: API responses often come in JSON format.*

---

## K

**Keylogger**  
Malicious software that records everything typed on a keyboard.  
*Example: A virus that captures passwords as you type them.*

**Keyword**  
A specific word or phrase used to search for information.  
*Example: Searching for "emergency contact" to find phone numbers.*

---

## L

**Leaked Data**  
Private information that has been accidentally or illegally released.  
*Example: A company's customer database posted on the dark web.*

**Linked Data**  
Information that connects different pieces of data together.  
*Example: Connecting an email address to social media accounts.*

---

## M

**Metadata**  
Hidden information about a file, such as when it was created or what camera took it.  
*Example: A Word document showing the author's name and edit history.*

**Mirror**  
An exact copy of a website or database stored elsewhere.  
*Example: Archive.org mirrors millions of websites for historical access.*

---

## N

**Nameserver**  
A computer that translates domain names into IP addresses.  
*Example: Telling browsers that google.com is at 142.250.80.46.*

---

## O

**Open Source**  
Information that is publicly available and can be accessed legally.  
*Example: Social media posts, public records, and websites.*

**OSINT (Open Source Intelligence)**  
Gathering information from publicly available sources to learn about a target.  
*Example: Using public records, social media, and websites to investigate a company.*

---

## P

**PII (Personally Identifiable Information)**  
Any information that could identify a specific person.  
*Example: Full name, Social Security number, home address, phone number.*

**Phishing**  
Fake emails or websites designed to steal login information or personal data.  
*Example: An email pretending to be from your bank asking you to "verify" your password.*

**Pivot**  
Using one piece of information to discover related information.  
*Example: Finding someone's email, then using it to find their social media accounts.*

**Progressive Disclosure**  
Showing simple information first, with options to see more detail.  
*Example: Showing a summary with a "more details" button.*

---

## R

**Reconnaissance (Recon)**  
The process of gathering information about a target before taking action.  
*Example: Researching a company online before a business meeting.*

**Red Flag**  
A warning sign that something may be wrong or suspicious.  
*Example: A job offer requiring you to pay money upfront is a red flag.*

**Reverse Lookup**  
Searching with an output to find the input (backwards from normal).  
*Example: Using a phone number to find who it belongs to.*

**Reverse Image Search**  
Using a photo to find where else it appears online.  
*Example: Uploading a dating profile photo to see if it's a stock image.*

**Robots.txt**  
A file that tells search engines which parts of a website they can visit.  
*Example: A website hiding /admin/ from Google but not from direct access.*

---

## S

**Scraper**  
A tool that automatically extracts information from websites.  
*Example: Collecting all email addresses from a company's website pages.*

**Screenshot**  
An image capture of what is displayed on a screen.  
*Example: Saving evidence of a website before it changes or is deleted.*

**Search Operator**  
Special commands that make search engines find specific results.  
*Example: Using `site:example.com` to only search within one website.*

**Security Header**  
Technical instructions that help protect websites from attacks.  
*Example: X-Frame-Options prevents a website from being embedded in malicious pages.*

**SIEM (Security Information and Event Management)**  
A system that collects and analyzes security alerts from various sources.  
*Example: Monitoring logs from firewalls, servers, and applications for threats.*

**Sock Puppet**  
A fake online identity used to deceive others.  
*Example: Creating a fake social media profile to spy on competitors.*

**Social Engineering**  
Manipulating people into revealing confidential information.  
*Example: Pretending to be IT support to get someone's password.*

**Spider**  
See "Crawler" - a program that automatically visits and catalogs websites.

**Subdomain**  
A section of a website with its own address.  
*Example: mail.google.com, support.apple.com, blog.company.com.*

---

## T

**Threat Actor**  
A person or group that poses a security threat.  
*Example: Hackers, scammers, or spies conducting malicious activities.*

**Threat Intelligence**  
Information about threats that helps organizations protect themselves.  
*Example: Knowing that a particular hacker group is targeting your industry.*

**Timeline**  
A chronological record of events or activities.  
*Example: Mapping when someone started jobs, moved locations, or posted online.*

**TLS/SSL**  
Security protocols that encrypt data sent between computers.  
*Example: The technology that makes the padlock appear in your browser.*

**Token**  
A piece of data used for authentication or verification.  
*Example: A temporary code sent to your phone to log in.*

**TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures)**  
The patterns of behavior used by threat actors.  
*Example: A scammer's typical methods for approaching victims.*

---

## U

**URL (Uniform Resource Locator)**  
The address of a webpage or resource on the internet.  
*Example: https://www.example.com/page.html*

**User Agent**  
Information your browser sends to websites identifying itself.  
*Example: "Mozilla/5.0 (Windows NT 10.0; Win64; x64)"*

---

## V

**Vector**  
A method or pathway used to attack or gain access.  
*Example: Email attachments are a common malware vector.*

**VPN (Virtual Private Network)**  
A service that hides your internet connection and location.  
*Example: Using a VPN to browse from a different country's location.*

**Vulnerability**  
A weakness in a system that could be exploited.  
*Example: A website that doesn't require passwords for admin access.*

---

## W

**Wayback Machine**  
A digital archive of the World Wide Web maintained by Archive.org.  
*Example: Viewing what Facebook.com looked like in 2005.*

**WHOIS**  
A protocol to query databases storing registered users of internet resources.  
*Example: Finding who owns a domain and when it was registered.*

---

## X

**XSS (Cross-Site Scripting)**  
An attack where malicious scripts are injected into trusted websites.  
*Example: A hacker adding JavaScript to steal cookies from website visitors.*

---

## Z

**Zero-Day**  
A security vulnerability that is unknown to the software vendor.  
*Example: A newly discovered bug that hackers can exploit before it's fixed.*
